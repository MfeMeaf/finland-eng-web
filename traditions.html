Finnish Traditions

INDEPENDENCE DAY

Finland's Independence Day on December 6 is about honoring the memory of those who lost their lives in the struggle for independence, both in the First and in the Second World War. Family members and friends gather to enjoy dinner at home or in a restaurant. Watching the live television broadcast from the president's independence reception and critically examining guests' costumes and behavior is a much-loved tradition these days. Some people also take part in a torchlight procession or visit the soldiers' graves in the cemeteries. Blue and white lights are placed on the window sills.

Vappen

The biggest festivity of the spring is the coat of arms, or Walpurgis Night. People of all ages in all parts of the country gather the night before the first of May for a carnival-like street party. Newly-baked and former students march through town wearing their white graduation hats and enjoying the approaching summer. But it's not just about balloons, streamers and champagne. Traditionally, the first of May has a political connection and even today the labor movement celebrates its international holiday on the first of May. Public speeches are then given by party members in almost every square in the major cities. Finnish coat of arms delicacies include the strudel, a kind of float-cooked pastry, and the monk, which is served with mead - yummy!

The Little Christmas season - a taste of Christmas

The Little Christmas parties can start as early as the beginning of November. It is the darkest time of the year and therefore needs to be refreshed with music, dance, Christmas lights and the traditional Christmas drink mulled wine. Santa himself can also show up at a little Christmas party!
